###Review Report of my work A review of the two students assesment of my model reveal some issues that require addressing and potential improvement of my model. These issues are raised as potential problems to address in my model due to their implications on model performance, reliability, and ethical considerations especially as it bothers on model fairness:

  1. Potential Biases from Demographic Variables:
    • Problem: Including protected classes and demographic variables like sex, race, ethnicity, and age might introduce biases that favor specific demographic groups, impacting the fairness and ethical implications of the models predictions.
    • Impact: Biased models can lead to discriminatory or unfair outcomes, affecting trust and reliability, especially in sensitive domains like loan decisions.
  2. Handling Missing Data Using na.omit:
    • Problem: Using na.omit to handle missing data may result in the removal of rows with any missing values, potentially reducing the available information for model training.
    • Impact: Reduced dataset size may lead to information loss and affect the models ability to generalize well to new data, impacting predictive performance.
  3. Class Imbalance Approach Using upSample:
    • Problem: Upsampling the minority class to handle class imbalance may introduce synthetic data that doesnt naturally exist in the dataset, potentially influencing the models learning process and generalization to new data.
    • Impact: Oversampling techniques might lead to overfitting, where the model performs well on training data but poorly on unseen data, reducing its real-world applicability.

Addressing these issues is crucial to enhance the models performance, reliability, and ethical soundness. Exploring alternative methods for handling missing data, re-evaluating the inclusion of protective classes, and considering different strategies for handling class imbalance could potentially mitigate these concerns and improve the models overall effectiveness.

##Removal of denial_reason.1 from the preditors Denial Reason 1 has a high correlation to the outcome variable action_taken. Hence including it as a variable used to predict action_taken is not ideal. Hence the need to remove it.

The rationale for removing Denial Reason 1 as a predictor for predicting the outcome variable action_taken lies in its high correlation with the said outcome. This high correlation poses challenges when utilizing it as a predictive variable:

  1. Correlation Impact: Since it has a high correlation with the outcome variable (action_taken), it can distort the predictive models efficacy. Such a strong relationship might lead to issues like multicollinearity, making it difficult to isolate the independent effect of other predictors on the outcome.

  2. Model Interpretability: Including a highly correlated variable like Denial Reason 1 could hinder the interpretability of the model. It might overshadow the impact of other predictors, making it challenging to discern their individual contributions to predicting action_taken.

  3. Redundancy: Since Denial Reason 1 is closely linked to the outcome variable, its inclusion might not add significant additional information to the model. Removing it could streamline the model without sacrificing predictive accuracy.

  4. Model Overfitting: In some cases, like the one at hand, a highly correlated predictor might lead to overfitting, where the model performs exceptionally well on the training data but fails to generalize to new, unseen data. Removing such variables can help mitigate overfitting issues.

Therefore, the decision to remove Denial Reason 1 as a predictor is based on the desire to enhance the models interpretability, mitigate multicollinearity, prevent overfitting, and ensure that other relevant predictors can exert their independent influence on predicting action_taken.

##Deploying AUC to assess my classification models performance: I decided to deploy a model assessment metric that will specifically help me identify my classification models ability to differentiate between classesspecifically, positive and negative oneson a scale from 0 to 1. I decided to use the Area Under the Curve (AUC).

An AUC of 1 signifies a flawless classifier that perfectly segregates positive and negative instances, while an AUC of 0.5 indicates chance-level performance with no discrimination ability. Values between 0.5 and 1 denote varying degrees of discriminatory power.

# Handle missing values for non-target columns
get_mode <- function(x) {
  unique_x <- unique(x)
  unique_x[which.max(tabulate(match(x, unique_x)))]
}

cols_to_impute <- setdiff(names(data), "action_taken")  # Exclude target column
for (col in cols_to_impute) {
  mode_val <- get_mode(data[[col]])
  data[[col]][is.na(data[[col]])] <- mode_val
}

# Convert action_taken to factor
data$action_taken <- as.factor(data$action_taken)

# Split data into training and testing sets
set.seed(123)  # for reproducibility
split_ratio <- 0.7  # 70% for training, 30% for testing
train_indices <- sample(1:nrow(data), split_ratio * nrow(data))
train_data <- data[train_indices, ]
test_data <- data[-train_indices, ]

# Downsampling the training data
train_data <- downSample(train_data, y = train_data$action_taken)

# Specify the formula 
formula <- action_taken ~ purchaser_type + preapproval + loan_amount +
  manufactured_home_secured_property_type + loan_type + loan_purpose +
  lien_status + debt_to_income_ratio + applicant_credit_score_type +
  co.applicant_credit_score_type + income + 
  conforming_loan_limit + derived_loan_product_type + derived_dwelling_category

# Build the random forest model
model <- randomForest(formula, data = train_data, ntree = 100, na.action = na.omit)

# Print a summary of the model
print(model)
